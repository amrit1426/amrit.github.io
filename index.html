The Story

Nine years on the ground before a single line of SQL.

Certified in Data Analytics by E&ICT Academy, IIT Guwahati. Based in India, working with teams across telecom and analytics.

Amrit's career didn't start in a notebook — it started on rollout sites. For nine years he coordinated nationwide telecom network deployments across Ericsson, Samsung, Hughes Communications, and Airtel projects, working across GSM, 4G, and 5G rollouts, VSAT deployments, and O&M operations involving dozens of field teams at once.

The job was rarely glamorous. It was tracking milestones across 50+ concurrent sites, chasing escalations to resolution, keeping SLA adherence visible to delivery managers, and maintaining the MIS reports that let project leads know what was actually happening on the ground. It meant sitting between RF, RAN, backhaul, logistics, and field installation teams — and making sure none of them were surprised by what the others were doing.

"Every operational decision I supported was really a data decision — I just didn't have the tools yet to see it that way."

That realization is what pulled Amrit into analytics. He picked up SQL, Python, Power BI, and machine learning — not as a career pivot away from his experience, but as a way of finally naming what the job had always been about. The years spent managing stakeholders, tracking KPIs, and handling escalations turned into an instinct for what actually matters in a dataset: not just what the numbers say, but what decision they're supposed to support.

Today that combination — operational fluency plus analytical rigor — shows up in how he approaches every project: understand the business first, then let the data confirm or challenge it.

Analytics Philosophy

How I approach a new dataset

The same sequence every time, whether it's 10,000 rows or 787,000.

01

Understand the business

Learn what decision the data is meant to support before touching a single column.

02

Explore the data

Structured EDA to find what's real, what's missing, and what's noise.

03

Build meaningful KPIs

Metrics tied to a decision, not vanity numbers on a dashboard.

04

Visualize insights

Charts and dashboards designed for the person who has to act on them.

05

Communicate findings

Plain-language takeaways a stakeholder can repeat back correctly.

06

Support decisions

The output is a recommendation, not just a report.
